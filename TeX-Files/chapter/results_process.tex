Process factors influencing post fortification methods have been discussed in five studies. The findings mainly address two key principles:
\begin{itemize}
\item Type of fortification substance applied and
\item Type of fortification method utilized
\end{itemize}
The results also indicate that the fortification vehicle and method can interact, thereby either weakening or strengthening the initial effect. Some studies examine both principle simultaneously, investigating not only a specific fortification vehicle but also the method by which fortification is utilized. For instance, \citet{noodle} demonstrated this with instant noodles fortified through the addition of catfish powder, where the pasta was either tray‑dried or deep‑fat‑fried. The combination of deep-fat frying and catfish powder addition resulted in the cooking loss, attributed to the leaching of water-soluble micronutrients, a process more likely to occur through the formation of starch-lipid complexes during storage \autocite{starch}. Consequently, these findings illustrate that the fortification vehicle and method should be regarded as separate processes but closely linked to each other.

\par

Rather than focusing on vehicle-method interactions, other studies have investigated isolated processing methods - specifically how modifications to the physical delivery system can prevent nutrient degradation. One such approach involves applying a coating to fortification granules, first introduced by \citet{li2coatsalt} to ensure sufficient micronutrient retention. They did basic research and first steps to consider physical properties of fortification granules to ensure sufficient micronutrient retention. Recent findings indicate that characteristics such as coating thickness, size and shape influence attrition. The authors suggest that attrition contributes to the resilience of micronutrients under harsh processing conditions, although its potential role in post-fortification mechanisms remains unclear. In this context, the findings of \citet{li3capsalt} provide valuable insights. The authors further examined the influence of physical properties and demonstrated that applying a soy stearin coating to ferric pyrophosphate is highly effective in preventing reactions with fortified iodine, which would otherwise lead to iodine loss. Ferric pyrophosphate is also advantageous in terms of consumer acceptance, making it an important fortification vehicle. The study showed that even under elevated temperature and humidity, both multifortified salt and double-fortified salt remained stable after six months when coated with soy stearin. Additionally,  among the multifortified salt,  the presence of zinc, which could otherwise accelerate iodine loss,  did not cause degradation.
\par
Beyond the specific fortification method, the choice of an appropriate fortification vehicle also significantly affects storage stability. This was demonstrated in a study on the fortification of gari, which contributed to a better understanding of the importance of selecting the right fortification vehicle. In \citeyear{gari}, \citeauthor{gari} aimed to find solutions for Vitamin A retention, given the vitamin’s low stability and the need for carefully chosen fortification strategies \autocite{vitaproblems}. The authors aimed to identify a feasible approach to Vitamin A fortification in West Africa and found that Red Palm oil is the best fortification strategy to ensure high vitamin A retention in gari, a cassava-based West African food product, when compared to retinyl palmitate or yellow cassava. Other studies have shown that vitamin A fortification vehicles such as retinyl palmitate or $\beta$-carotene degrade rapidly under ambient conditions. Red Palm oil presumably has more stability due to its natural antioxidant content, as noted by the authors. In addition, cereal bran has been shown to function as a stabilising agent. These findings were observed under accelerated storage conditions, but the literature suggests that they are also applicable under ambient conditions.
